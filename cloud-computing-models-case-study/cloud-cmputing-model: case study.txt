Question 1
a). Evaluation of the IaaS Shared Responsibility Model
The Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS) shared responsibility model establishes a clear
division of duties in which the cloud provider is responsible for the security “of” the cloud,
while the consumer is responsible for the security “in” the cloud (A Cloud Guru, 2023).
This distinction is fundamental in transitioning from traditional infrastructure ownership
to a virtualized service model.
Referencing the IaaS Component Stack, the five layers strictly managed by the provider
include:
1. Physical Facilities – Data centres, including power, cooling, and physical security
2. Server Hardware – Physical compute hosts and processing units
3. Storage Devices – Physical disks and storage infrastructure
4. Networking – Physical routers, switches, and cabling
5. Virtualization Layer – Hypervisors enabling virtual machine abstraction
These layers align with definitions provided by the National Institute of Standards and
Technology (2011) and conceptual frameworks by Thomas Erl et al. (2013).
The provider’s management of these layers enables Service Abstraction, which refers to
the concealment of complex infrastructure operations behind simplified, softwaredefined
interfaces (Erl et al., 2013). Through virtualization, physical resources are
transformed into on-demand virtual components, such as virtual machines, storage
volumes, and virtual networks.
This abstraction:
 Eliminates the need for hardware procurement and maintenance
 Removes responsibility for physical security and environmental controls
 Enables rapid provisioning and scalability
 Reduces capital expenditure in favour of operational expenditure
b). Consumer Responsibilities and Operational Liabilities under the SPI Model
In contrast to the provider-managed layers, the SPI (Software–Platform–Infrastructure)
model assigns significant responsibility to TechStream’s internal team for managing the
upper layers of the stack. These include:
1. Guest Operating System
2. Middleware (web servers, database systems)
3. Runtime Environments (programming frameworks)
4. Applications (custom-developed services)
5. Data (sensitive financial and user information)
According to the National Institute of Standards and Technology (2011), IaaS enables
consumers to deploy and run arbitrary software, thereby granting them full
administrative control over these layers.
This creates several operational liabilities, including:
 Operating System Management: Regular patching, updates, and vulnerability
mitigation
 Security Configuration: Managing firewalls, access controls, and antivirus
systems (A Cloud Guru, 2023)
 Middleware Maintenance: Ensuring compatibility and performance optimisation
 Application Management: Debugging, scaling, and maintaining uptime
 Data Governance: Ensuring encryption, backups, and regulatory compliance
These layers are categorized as “managed by consumers” because they are closely
tied to business-specific logic, security policies, and regulatory requirements, which
cannot be standardized by the provider without limiting flexibility (NIST, 2011).
In a high-stakes production environment, the risks of mismanagement are substantial:
 Increased security vulnerabilities due to unpatched systems
 Potential data breaches from misconfigured access controls
 Regulatory non-compliance with financial data protection laws
 System downtime, affecting customer trust and revenue
 Data integrity failures, impacting decision-making and operations
As noted by Erl et al. (2013), while IaaS provides flexibility and scalability, it also
introduces increased management complexity an d requires strong governance from the
consumer side.
Question 2
a). Comparison of cloud delivery models and Technical progression of
abstraction
In a comparison of cloud delivery models, Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) provides a
significantly higher degree of “implementation hiding” than Platform-as-a-Service (PaaS)
(Erl et al., 2013). While PaaS abstracts the underlying infrastructure (such as servers
and operating systems), the consumer is still responsible for the implementation and
management of the application code itself (NIST, 2011; Erl et al., 2013). In contrast,
SaaS achieves maximum service abstraction by hiding not only the infrastructure but
also the entire application logic and its technical implementation, leaving the user with
only limited application configuration settings (NIST, 2011; Erl et al., 2013).
The technical progression of abstraction intensifies as an organization moves through
the following layers:
 IaaS Foundation: This provides the lowest level of abstraction, where the
provider hides only the physical hardware and virtualization layer, requiring the
consumer to manage everything from the operating system upward ( NIST, 2018;
A Cloud Guru, 2023).
 PaaS Elevation: As the organization moves to PaaS, the level of abstraction
increases to include the middleware, runtimes, and the operating system,
allowing the consumer to focus purely on application deployment and code
( NIST, 2018; Google Cloud, 2023).
 SaaS Delivery: At this final stage, the level of abstraction is at its highest, as the
provider packages the entire software solution into a “ready-to-use” product,
completely relieving the consumer of any technical or operational implementation
details (NIST, 2011, Erl et al., 2013).
b). Long-Term Impact on software lifecycle management when transitioning from
PaaS to SaaS
The long-term impact on software lifecycle management for a team like DevFlow shifts
dramatically when moving from a platform-focused model to a SaaS model. Under a
Platform-as-a-Service model like Google App Engine, DevFlow is relieved of
infrastructure administration, such as provisioning and resource management, but they
retain full responsibility for the application lifecycle, including code updates and
application-level security ( NIST, 2011; Google Cloud, 2023). While Google handles the
underlying platform patches, DevFlow must still manage the "long-term headache" of
maintaining their custom-built software (Google Cloud, 2023).
When moving to a SaaS model like Salesforce, this responsibility shifts significantly to
the provider. The SaaS provider is strictly responsible for all critical tasks, including
security patches, application upgrades, and infrastructure maintenance (Erl et al., 2013;
NIST, 2018 ). This transition eliminates DevFlow’s need for an internal team to manage
software versions or bug fixes, as the provider manages the entire application stack on
their behalf (NIST, 2011; Google Cloud, 2023 ). Consequently, the operational liability
for software performance and reliability moves from DevFlow to the cloud provider, as
governed by the Service Level Agreement (SLA) ( Erl et al., 2013; A Cloud Guru, 2023).
Question 3
a). Community Cloud deployment and Cost-Benefits of Public and Cloud Security
Boundaries of Private Cloud
A Community Cloud deployment is the optimal choice for this university consortium
because it is specifically designed for exclusive use by a community of consumers with
shared concerns, such as a common mission, security requirements, and regulatory
compliance policies (NIST SP 800-145; NIST SP 500-322). The specific “Common
Purpose” justifying this restricted-access model is the collaborative hosting of sensitive
research, genomic sequencing and socioeconomic data modeling, which necessitates
strict adherence to local data protection regulations, such as the Protection of Personal
Information Act, 2013 (POPIA), which general public environments may not satisfy
(Protection of Personal Information Act, 2013).
This model successfully bridges the gap between public and private deployments by
combining their respective strengths:
 Cost-Benefits of Public Cloud: Like a public cloud, a community cloud
leverages resource pooling, allowing the universities to benefit from economies
of scale and proportional costs by sharing a common infrastructure (NIST, 2011;
Erl et al., 2013). This reduces the need for each institution to make significant upfront
capital investments in individual HPC hardware (NIST, 2011).
 Security Boundaries of Private Cloud: Unlike a public cloud that is open to the
public, a community cloud provides heightened privacy and security (NIST, 2011).
By restricting access to a specific, known set of participating organizations, it
creates a more controlled trust boundary, ensuring that sensitive research data is
handled within an environment that mimics the exclusivity and governance of a
private cloud (NIST, 2011; Erl et al., 2013).
b). Comparison of Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) and Application Service
Provider (ASP)- Multi-tenancy and Scalability
Modern Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) delivery represents a significant technical and
economic evolution over the 1990s Application Service Provider (ASP) or "Hosted
Application" models, primarily through its approach to multi-tenancy and scalability:
 Multi-tenancy: Legacy ASP models often relied on single-tenant architectures
where each customer required separate, siloed resources, leading to significant
complexities related to upkeep and high initial expenses (IJITCS, 2024). In
contrast, modern SaaS is built on a multi-tenant model where the provider shares
a pool of configurable computing resources among multiple customers (NIST
2011; Google Cloud, 2023). This allows the consortium to share computing
resources more efficiently through logical isolation, as the provider dynamically
assigns and reassigns physical and virtual resources according to the demand of
each university (NIST 2011).
 Scalability: While 1990s models were limited by the fixed capacity of physical
hardware, modern SaaS utilizes rapid elasticity (Buyya et al., 2009; NIST, 2011).
This characteristic allows the HPC infrastructure to scale "outward and inward"
automatically and nearly instantaneously to meet the massive processing
demands of genomic sequencing (NIST, 2011). To the academic consortium,
these resources appear unlimited and can be appropriated in any quantity at any
time without the traditional "management headaches" of manual capacity
planning (NIST, 2011; Erl et al., 2013).
Economically, modern SaaS is superior because it replaces anticipated capital
expenditures with measured service (Erl et al., 2013; Google Cloud, 2023). The
consortium only pays for what it consumes, achieving economic agility that the legacy
ASP approach, which often required rigid, long-term contracts for fixed capacity could
not provide (NIST 2011; Erl et al., 2013).
